FAQS

Where will you prepare my placenta pills?

I can prepare at my home or at yours. It’s your choice and there is no price difference. If you want more privacy or are concerned about the smell, it’s be tter for me to prepare at my home. If you are curious about the process and want to watch, then it is better for me to prepare at your home.

How long will the process take?

After I start the process, your placenta products will be delivered or ready for pick-up 72 hours or less.

I’m delivering at home, what do I need to know?

Make sure you have gallon ziplock bags on hand and a sealing bowl for refrigerator storage. Also tell your midwife you will be keeping your placenta. Make sure the placenta is immediately placed in the refrigerator or on ice after delivery and inspection.

I’m delivering at a hospital or birth center, what do I need to know?

Take a small, hard-sided cooler with you to the birth location, as well as a few gallon ziplock bags. You will need to tell the birthing staff before deliv ering that you want to keep your placenta and write it into your birth plan. It is also a good idea to have a designated “placenta person” to keep track of the placenta to make su re that after the delivery and inspection of the placenta, it is promptly packaged and refrigerated or put on ice in a cooler. The staff might put your placenta in a sealed contai ner, which is great for transport. If the container is too big for your cooler, ask to have the placenta transferred to your ziplock bags (double bagged). Make sure that your plac enta never leaves your room. This is a good precaution to take to make sure that your placenta doesn’t get lost or accidentally discarded. If you forget your cooler, it is fine to put the sealed placenta on ice in the small plastic tub the hospital sends home with you.

Is there any reason my placenta can’t be encapsulated?

If your placenta is sent to the Pathology Lab for testing, or infection of the placenta or amniotic fluid, I will be unable to prepare your placenta. Signs of infection in the mother during labor, may mean that consuming your placenta could be harmful to your health. I will always consult with your care provider before making a choi ce on whether or not your placenta is viable for ingestion. If your provider wants to send the placenta to Pathology, discuss with them if it is possible to send only a small piec e to be examined, not the whole placenta.

What if I am induced, have a medicated birth, or have a cesarean section?

It is still 100% fine to encapsulate!

What is the difference between the raw and steamed methods?

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, birth is thought to leave behind a lot of cold, open spaces. This is considered yin. Because the goal is always to have yi n and yang (heat and cold) in balance, the postpartum remedy includes using a steamed placenta. Steamed method yields fewer capsules, but is a more calming choice, as well as a sa fer choice in regards to bacteria. The raw method is not just raw placenta stuffed into capsules; don’t worry. It is raw placenta that has been sliced and dehydrated. It retains m ore of its nutrients and hormones since it isn’t steamed. Since raw foods are energizing, it can bring energy to your postpartum period. This method isn’t a good choice for moms w ho already suffer from anxiety or insomnia because the energizing qualities have a potential to make anxiety increase. Possibly the best way to consume raw placenta, however, is t o blend a small cube into a smoothie. This is especially helpful immediately after birth, because it will aid in lessening bleeding. But I realize this isn’t something every woman can handle! Which method should you choose? Think about the postpartum you want to have. Do you want to feel calm and balanced or energized? Follow your instincts!
